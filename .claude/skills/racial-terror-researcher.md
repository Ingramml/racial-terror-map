**Major Digitized Newspaper Collections:**

- **Library of Congress - Chronicling America**
  - https://chroniclingamerica.loc.gov/
  - Free access to historical US newspapers (1777-1963)
  - Searchable by keyword, date, location
  - Best for: Pre-1963 incidents

- **ProQuest Historical Newspapers**
  - Often available through university libraries
  - Major papers: New York Times, Washington Post, Chicago Tribune
  - Advanced search capabilities
  - Best for: Major urban incidents

- **Newspapers.com**
  - Largest collection of digitized newspapers
  - Subscription required (sometimes free trials)
  - Best for: Local/regional coverage

- **Google News Archive**
  - https://news.google.com/newspapers
  - Free access to selected historical papers
  - Coverage varies by region

- **Internet Archive**
  - https://archive.org/details/newspapers
  - Free digitized newspaper collections
  - Includes Black press archives

**Black Press Archives (Critical for Counter-Narratives):**

- **Chicago Defender Archive**
  - https://www.proquest.com/chicagodefender
  - Premier Black newspaper (1910-1975)
  - Essential for accurate reporting of racial violence

- **Pittsburgh Courier Archive**
  - Major Black newspaper
  - Strong coverage of Southern violence

- **Baltimore Afro-American**
  - Extensive civil rights era coverage

- **The Crisis (NAACP Magazine)**
  - https://www.crisismagazine.com/
  - Annual lynching reports and racial violence documentation

## Search Strategy Framework

### Step 1: Incident Identification

**Start with known incident lists:**
1. Check existing datasets (Wikipedia, EJI, Racial Violence Archive)
2. Identify: Location, approximate date, incident type
3. Note key figures: victims, perpetrators, locations

### Step 2: Newspaper Search Terms

**Effective search queries:**

**For specific incidents:**
```
"[City Name]" AND "riot" AND [Year]
"[Church/Building Name]" AND "bombing"
"[Victim Name]" AND "lynching"
```

**For discovery research:**
```
"Negro" AND "mob" AND [City] (historical term usage)
"colored" AND "violence" AND [State]
"race riot" AND [Year range]
"church bombing" AND [City]
"massacre" AND "Black" AND [Location]
```

**Black Press searches (often more accurate):**
```
"racial terrorism" AND [Location]
"white mob" AND [City]
"lynched" AND [County]
```

### Step 3: Cross-Reference Sources

**Verify details across multiple sources:**
- [ ] White mainstream press (often underreported deaths)
- [ ] Black press (more accurate casualty counts)
- [ ] Academic historical analysis
- [ ] Government records (if available)

**Note discrepancies:**
- Official death toll vs. estimated toll
- Contemporary reports vs. historical analysis
- Perpetrator identification (often protected by police)

### Step 4: Data Extraction

**Key information to collect:**

**Essential Fields:**
- [ ] Event name/description
- [ ] Date (exact or approximate)
- [ ] City, State
- [ ] Latitude/Longitude (use geocoding tools)
- [ ] Official death toll
- [ ] Estimated/actual death toll (if different)
- [ ] Number injured
- [ ] Type (massacre, riot, bombing, lynching, arson)

**Contextual Fields:**
- [ ] Narrative (2-3 sentence summary)
- [ ] Trigger/cause
- [ ] Perpetrators (KKK, white mob, police, etc.)
- [ ] Victims (demographic info)
- [ ] Property destroyed
- [ ] Legal outcome (arrests, convictions)
- [ ] Reference URLs

## Advanced Research Techniques

### 1. Discovering Unknown Incidents

**Use aggregated incident lists as starting points:**
- EJI's county-by-county lynching reports
- Wikipedia's list of race riots
- Academic books with appendices (e.g., "The Blood of Emmett Till")

**Scan regional newspapers for patterns:**
- Search "[State]" AND "racial violence" by decade
- Look for "race trouble" (euphemism in white press)
- Check Black press for "mob violence" reports

### 2. Handling Incomplete Records

**When official records are missing:**
- Check Black press (often more complete)
- Look for NAACP field reports
- Search academic dissertations on local history
- Contact local historical societies

**Death toll discrepancies:**
- Official toll: Usually from white authorities (undercount)
- Black press estimates: Often higher, more accurate
- Academic consensus: Use footnotes to find sources
- **Best practice:** Record both official and estimated tolls

### 3. Geographic Precision

**Getting accurate coordinates:**
- Use Google Maps for modern addresses
- Historical buildings: Check National Register of Historic Places
- Churches: Search church name + city in Google Maps
- Demolished sites: Use historical maps or city directories

**Tools:**
- https://www.latlong.net/ (quick geocoding)
- Google Maps (right-click ‚Üí "What's here?")
- OpenStreetMap (historical features sometimes preserved)

## Source Evaluation Checklist

**Assessing reliability:**

- [ ] **Primary source?** (newspaper from the time vs. later analysis)
- [ ] **Author bias?** (white mainstream vs. Black press perspective)
- [ ] **Corroboration?** (Do multiple sources confirm details?)
- [ ] **Official vs. actual?** (Government records often minimize Black deaths)
- [ ] **Perpetrator accountability?** (Were arrests made? Convictions?)

**Red flags for unreliable sources:**
- Wikipedia without citations
- Single-source claims (especially death tolls)
- Rounded numbers without explanation (e.g., "about 100 dead")
- Racist language without historical context

## Ethical Considerations

**When researching racial violence:**

1. **Centering Black voices:**
   - Prioritize Black press and scholarship
   - Include victim names when known and appropriate
   - Avoid sensationalizing violence

2. **Accurate terminology:**
   - Use "racial terrorism" not "race riot" when white mobs attacked Black communities
   - Use "massacre" for one-sided mass killings
   - Avoid euphemisms like "racial disturbance"

3. **Acknowledging gaps:**
   - Many incidents were never documented
   - Black deaths often uncounted or minimized
   - Perpetrators rarely prosecuted

4. **Respecting descendants:**
   - This is recent history - many survivors and descendants are living
   - Treat victims with dignity
   - Acknowledge ongoing impact of racial terror
